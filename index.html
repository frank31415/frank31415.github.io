Thomas Sowell

Thomas Sowell is the Rose and Milton Friedman Senior Fellow on Public Policy at the Hoover Institution.

Here's a timeline of Thomas Sowell's life:

  • - Born in Gastonia, North Carolina
  • Both of his parents died when he was a child and he was raised in poverty by his great-aunt.
  • - Moved to Harlem, New York City
  • Attended Stuyvesant High School in New York City, but had to drop out at age 17 because of financial difficulties.
  • - Born in Gastonia, North Carolina
  • - During the Korean War he got drafted into the military and joined the U.S. Marine Corps.
  • - Graduated magna cum laude from Harvard University with a Bachlor degree in economics.
  • Sowell said that in his twenties he was a Marxist. But after working for the US department of Labor from 1961 until 1962 he began to change his mind toward favouring free market economics, after having studied the impact of minimum wages on unemployement in the sugar industry in Puerto Rico.
  • - Earned a PhD in economics from the University of Chicago, where he studied under Milton Friedman.
  • Taught economics at many universities, including Cornell University, Howard University, Rutgers, Brandeis University and UCLA.
  • - Became a Senior Fellow at the Hoover Institution at Stanford University.
  • - Published the book: "A Conflict of Visions: Ideological Origins of Political Struggles". Thomas Sowell continued to published many more books on topic such as social policy on race, ethnic groups, education, and decision-making, and classical and Marxian economics. Some of the most notable publications are included in this timeline.
  • - Published: "Race And Culture: A World View"
  • - Published: "Basic Economics: A Citizen's Guide to the Economy"
  • - Published: "Black Rednecks and White Liberals"
  • - Published: "Economic Facts and Fallacies"
  • - Published: "Discrimination and Disparities"
  • On many occasions, Thomas Sowell made it clear that he opposes policies like affirmative action, minimum wage, the welfare state and market regulations and made clear that often policies with good intentions can have negative effects on the very same people they were supposed to help.

"The first lesson of economics is scarcity: there is never enough of anything to fully satisfy all those who want it. The first lesson of politics is to disregard the first lesson of economics."

"Sometimes it seems as if there are more solutions than problems. On closer scrutiny, it turns out that many of today's problems are a result of yesterday's solutions."
